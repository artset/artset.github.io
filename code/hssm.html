Context

Emojis are an interesting phenomenon of language, given they are universally used throughout many languagse. Due to this aspect of emojis, I worked with 3 other peers, Minna, Maggie, and Iris, to explore the usage of emojis across different languages. This was a free flowing exploration to see the nuances of how different languages used emojis through sentiment analysis and context.

Initial Explorations in Sentiment

Gathering our data from Twitter, we scraped over 250,000 tweets over the course of two months. We queried the Twitter API for emojis of different categories, then placed the data in a SQL database. We were curious about sentiment analysis, so we used BlobText to classify the sentiment of each tweet as we queried Twitter. Then, we applied the kmeans algorithm to create clustering for each of the words.

Our initial visualization in MatPlotlib, which we used to cluster our three emojis into three categories -- positive (red), neutral(yellow), and negative(blue), indicated by the color in the bottom right. We had a lot of difficulties rendering the emojis in their full glory in MatPlotlib, which led us to use d3 in our later visualizations.

As seen from these visualizatons to the right, the BlobText API classified most tweets as neutral. We decided that the information that BlobText gave us was not particularly useful. As a result, we decided to pivot our focus to the specific context these emojis were used, rather than just sentiment.

Context Vectors

We implemented an algorithm that mapped each word to its sparse word vector, then calculated the cosine distance from emojis to each word. We chose four fairly popular emojis (happy, sad, sleepy, mad) and the top used languages (English, Japanese, Spanish) in our database. We found some interesting nuances, such as Japan's sad emoji had some positive words, such as beautiful, and their tired emojis would relate to academia, which wasn't found in the other languages. We also explored predicting the sentiment of a tweet given the emojis in the tweet, using SVMs and decision trees.
